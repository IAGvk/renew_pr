A flexible hydrogen electrolyser is a water-splitting device designed to rapidly adjust its electricity consumption in response to fluctuating renewable energy sources (like wind and solar) while producing hydrogen. Unlike traditional, inflexible, or "baseload" systems that must run constantly, flexible electrolysers can ramp up or down within seconds or minutes.

Key Characteristics of Flexible Electrolysers
Rapid Response Time: They can start, stop, and adjust output in seconds or minutes, allowing them to follow volatile power inputs.
Load Balancing & Grid Services: They help stabilize power grids by providing frequency control and alleviating congestion.
High Dynamic Range: They can operate efficiently across a wide range of power inputs (low minimum operating output).
Modular Design: Often built in containers for easier, smaller-scale deployment at locations like fueling stations, reducing the need for transporting hydrogen.

Primary Technologies for Flexible H₂ 
While all electrolysers split water, certain types are better suited for flexibility: 
Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM): Known for their high speed in ramping up and down, making them ideal for fluctuating renewable sources.
Anion Exchange Membrane (AEM): A developing technology that combines the low cost of alkaline with the flexibility and high performance of PEM.
Battolyser: A specialized type that acts as both a battery and an electrolyser, allowing for immediate response to power changes.

Benefits
Reduced Costs: Operators can run the electrolyser only when electricity prices are low, which can increase profitability by up to 60% compared to 24/7 operation.
Increased Efficiency: They minimize the need for, or cost of, grid curtailment, ensuring more renewable energy is utilized.
Green Hydrogen Production: By running on renewable energy, these systems ensure that the produced hydrogen has a near-zero carbon footprint.

Applications
Flexible electrolysers are crucial for:
Hard-to-abate sectors: Producing hydrogen for industries like steel and chemical production.
Decentralized Production: Providing hydrogen for refueling stations, allowing for localized, on-demand production.
Long-Duration Energy Storage: Converting excess energy to hydrogen, which can be stored for days or weeks.
